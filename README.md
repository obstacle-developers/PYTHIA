# PYTHIA

**Early research software for auditable particle-physics anomaly reasoning**

PYTHIA is an early research software package for auditable particle-physics anomaly reasoning. It has completed a Week 12 reasoning pipeline and a Week 13 mock anomaly-family workflow with safety-first reporting and a reproducible demo capsule.

PYTHIA does **not** claim particle discovery. It reports candidate patterns, unresolved anomaly families, insufficient evidence, and human review required. All outputs are audit-oriented and intended to support transparent review, not autonomous discovery decisions.

## What PYTHIA does

PYTHIA is not a classifier, chatbot, or black-box discovery authority. It is a scientific reasoning infrastructure that emphasizes provenance, traceability, conservative wording, and human review.

The current verified workflow can:

- assemble auditable reasoning traces for selected anomaly cases;
- connect observations to evidence, phenomenology, candidate explanations, constraints, rankings, recommendations, and source bundles;
- evaluate reasoning-trace completeness and consistency;
- group Week 13 mock anomaly traces into unresolved anomaly-family reports;
- emit conservative audit records for clusters, members, consistency checks, and reports;
- preserve insufficient-evidence language whenever evidence is incomplete or weakening conditions appear.

Every conclusion is intended to remain traceable to explicit workflow inputs and recorded reasoning steps.

---

## Safe-language policy

PYTHIA uses safety-first scientific language:

- no autonomous particle-discovery claims;
- no statements that establish evidence for new physics by themselves;
- candidate patterns are reported as patterns requiring review;
- unresolved anomaly families remain unresolved until known explanations and checks are exhausted;
- insufficient evidence is stated when support is incomplete;
- human review is required for all audit outputs.

## Core architecture principles

1. No LLM decides physics truth.
2. Every rejection requires provenance.
3. Store rejected theories; they are scientific results.
4. No hidden reasoning.
5. Constraints are data.
6. Evaluator is code.
7. Every reasoning step must be auditable.
8. Retrieval scores are relevance, not probability.
9. Ranking scores are ordering, not probability.
10. Recommendations suggest follow-up, not discovery claims.
11. Human review is required.
